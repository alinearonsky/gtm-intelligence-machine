# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Non-technical go-to-market people — founders, marketers, and GTM leads — at
healthtech B2B vendors selling into digital health and clinical software (the
first lens is Semedy's Terminology Tool). Their context: they open the dashboard
to decide **who to reach out to, and when**, without reading raw hiring data or
running queries themselves. They are commercially sharp but not technical; the
tool has to do the interpretation for them.

## Product Purpose

Turns healthtech companies' hiring signals into a prioritized, human-readable
feed of accounts entering a buying window. The daily scan reads job postings,
extracts and interprets signals (e.g. "actively building clinical-terminology
capability"), scores each company's priority, and presents one card per company
with a plain-English rationale, the underlying signals, the evidence behind them,
and a ready-to-send outreach brief. Success is simple: the user opens it and
within seconds knows the two or three companies worth contacting this week and
why — no analysis on their part.

## Brand Personality

Calm, precise, trustworthy. A quiet operator's console, not a marketing surface:
it earns confidence through legibility and restraint rather than flourish. Voice
is plain and exact — it names things the way a knowledgeable colleague would, and
never dresses up a weak signal as a strong one. Visual reference point: the
Stellate console (cool navy chrome, periwinkle-indigo, hairline slate, one warm
coral for "act now").

## Anti-references

- **Generic SaaS slop** — gradient accents, identical card grids, the
  hero-metric template, purple-blue-everything. The default AI dashboard look.
- **Vanity-metric dashboards** — big flashy numbers and charts for their own
  sake that look busy but never tell you who to act on.
- **Enterprise-dense CRM (Salesforce)** — joyless, overloaded, every pixel packed
  with controls and a steep learning curve. This tool is for a non-technical user.

## Design Principles

1. **Who to act on, and why — in one glance.** Priority is the organizing spine
   of every screen; the highest-value company is always the easiest to find.
2. **Signal over decoration.** Color, weight, and emphasis appear only where they
   carry meaning. The one warm accent (coral) means "act now" and nothing else.
3. **Humanize the machine.** The database stores slugs; the UI never shows one.
   Every machine value passes through a human label so the product reads like a
   colleague wrote it, not a query.
4. **Trust through evidence.** Every signal is traceable to the posting that fired
   it. The user can always see why this company, and why now.
5. **Calm density.** Dense enough to be useful in one screen, calm enough for a
   non-technical user — neither a bare landing page nor an enterprise cockpit.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG 2.1 AA: body text ≥ 4.5:1 against its background, large text ≥ 3:1.
Honor `prefers-reduced-motion` for every animation (crossfade or instant
fallback). Priority is carried by an explicit label as well as hue, so the
coral / blue / grey system does not rely on color alone.
